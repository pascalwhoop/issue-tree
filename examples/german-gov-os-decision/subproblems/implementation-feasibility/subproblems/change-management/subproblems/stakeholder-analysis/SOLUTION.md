# Solution: Stakeholder Mapping and Concerns

## Answer

The German government OS decision landscape involves a complex multi-layered stakeholder ecosystem across federal, state (Länder), and local levels, with varying power dynamics and competing interests. Key stakeholder groups can be mapped into four categories based on their power/influence and interest levels:

**High Power, High Interest (Manage Closely):**
- Federal IT-Planungsrat (IT Planning Council)
- BSI (Federal Office for Information Security)
- Federal and State CIOs/Digital Ministers
- State governments (16 Länder)
- Federal data protection commissioners

**High Power, Lower Interest (Keep Satisfied):**
- Coalition political leadership
- Federal Ministry of Interior and Digital Affairs
- Finance ministries (budget authority)
- Large IT vendors (Microsoft, SAP, Oracle)

**Lower Power, High Interest (Keep Informed):**
- IT staff and administrators across all levels
- Works councils and trade unions (especially ver.di)
- Municipal IT managers
- Open source advocates and digital sovereignty proponents

**Lower Power, Lower Interest (Monitor):**
- Individual end users
- Citizens/general public
- Small vendors and consultants

## Evidence

### Federal Level Stakeholders

**1. IT-Planungsrat (IT Planning Council)**
- **Role**: Central political steering body for digitalization across federal-state boundaries
- **Composition**: Federal Government Commissioner for IT, State Secretaries (State-level CIOs), Federal Commissioner for Data Protection, and representatives of municipal associations
- **Power**: High - decisions are binding for federal and state digitalisation activities
- **Interest**: Very High - directly responsible for IT interoperability, security standards, and eGovernment projects
- **Concerns**: Digital sovereignty, coordination complexity across 16 states, standardization vs. autonomy balance
- **Motivation**: Effective governance, cost efficiency, political accountability

**2. BSI (Bundesamt für Sicherheit in der Informationstechnik)**
- **Role**: Federal cybersecurity authority and IT security service provider
- **Power**: Very High - develops mandatory security standards (IT-Grundschutz), certifies systems, can compel IT companies to provide security information
- **Interest**: Very High - responsible for protecting federal IT systems and setting security requirements
- **Concerns**: Security vulnerabilities from monoculture, vendor lock-in risks, foreign government access (US Cloud Act), supply chain security
- **Motivation**: National security, cyber resilience, reducing strategic vulnerability
- **Evidence**: In 2024, BSI pressured Microsoft to release white paper on encryption implementation, demonstrating regulatory authority

**3. Federal CIO and New Digital Ministry (2025)**
- **Role**: Since May 2025, Federal Ministry for Digital Transformation and Government Modernisation consolidates previously dispersed digital responsibilities
- **Power**: High - controls federal IT systems, ITZBund, federal cloud operations, digital strategy
- **Interest**: Very High - directly accountable for digital transformation success
- **Concerns**: Vendor dependency (cloud costs jumped from €136M in 2021 to €344M in 2024), coordination across ministries, political visibility of failures
- **Motivation**: Political legacy, modernization success, cost control
- **Evidence**: Ministry creation demonstrates elevated political priority for digital issues

**4. ITZBund (Information Technology Center BUND)**
- **Role**: Federal government's central IT service provider, evolving into "multi-cloud manager"
- **Power**: Medium-High (operational rather than strategic)
- **Interest**: High - responsible for implementing whatever OS strategy is chosen
- **Concerns**: Technical feasibility, migration complexity, service continuity, staff retraining needs
- **Motivation**: Operational excellence, avoiding service disruptions, professional reputation

**5. Federal Data Protection Commissioner (BfDI)**
- **Role**: Supervisory authority for federal public bodies, member of IT Planning Council
- **Power**: High - can block non-compliant IT decisions
- **Interest**: High - data protection implications of OS and cloud choices
- **Concerns**: US Cloud Act allowing foreign government data access, GDPR compliance, data sovereignty
- **Motivation**: Privacy protection, legal compliance, limiting surveillance risk
- **Evidence**: Position evolved on Microsoft 365 - Hessian DPA approved it in Nov 2025 after Microsoft addressed seven critical deficiencies

**6. Federal Ministries and Agencies**
- **Power**: Medium-High (collectively), Medium (individually)
- **Interest**: Medium - each has own IT priorities and legacy systems
- **Concerns**: Budget allocation, operational disruption, ministry-specific application compatibility
- **Motivation**: Mission continuity, avoiding negative headlines, maintaining autonomy

### State (Länder) Level Stakeholders

**7. 16 State Governments**
- **Role**: Autonomous IT decision-makers within federal coordination framework
- **Power**: Very High within their jurisdiction - Article 30 of Basic Law grants states authority except where federal jurisdiction specified
- **Interest**: High to Very High - varies by state
- **Concerns**: Autonomy preservation, cost burden, political risk of failed migrations
- **Motivation**: Varies by state - some prioritize digital sovereignty (Schleswig-Holstein), others prefer stability and vendor support
- **Evidence**: Schleswig-Holstein's independent decision to migrate 30,000 PCs to Linux demonstrates state autonomy in practice

**8. State CIOs and IT Agencies**
- **Role**: Participate in IT Planning Council, implement state IT strategies
- **Power**: High at state level
- **Interest**: Very High - directly responsible for state IT operations
- **Concerns**: Coordination with municipal level, varying IT maturity across states, federal-state alignment
- **Motivation**: Successful digital service delivery, cost efficiency, avoiding high-profile failures

**Key Case Study - Schleswig-Holstein:**
- **Decision**: Migrating 30,000 employees to Linux/LibreOffice by end of 2026
- **Champion**: Minister for Digitalisation Dirk Schrödter
- **Primary Motivation**: Data protection, digital sovereignty, cost savings
- **Status**: 80% complete as of late 2025, with email migration (100M+ emails) completed Oct 2025
- **Political Backing**: Confirmed by Minister-President Daniel Günther
- **Stakeholder Assessment**: Considered "major success" by state leadership

### Local Level Stakeholders

**9. Municipal Associations and Local Government**
- **Role**: Represent 11,000+ municipalities, included in IT Planning Council through representatives
- **Power**: Low individually, Medium collectively through associations
- **Interest**: High - municipalities deliver most citizen-facing digital services
- **Concerns**: Severely limited budgets and skilled personnel, lack of explicit inclusion in federal e-government laws, massive implementation gap (only 100 of 700 services digitized by end 2022)
- **Motivation**: Improved service delivery without budget increases, reduced administrative burden
- **Evidence**: Only €263M of €3.1B in 2025 digital spending directed to local digitalization despite municipalities handling most citizen services

**10. Municipal IT Managers**
- **Power**: Low
- **Interest**: High - directly responsible for local IT operations
- **Concerns**: Resource constraints, interoperability with state/federal systems, vendor support availability locally
- **Motivation**: Practical solutions that work with limited resources, avoiding complex migrations

### Cross-Cutting Stakeholder Groups

**11. IT Staff and System Administrators**
- **Role**: Day-to-day IT operations across all government levels
- **Power**: Low-Medium (can slow/sabotage implementation through resistance)
- **Interest**: Very High - directly affects their work
- **Concerns**: Job security, skill requirements changing, training adequacy, workload during migration, professional reputation if systems fail
- **Motivation**: Career preservation, professional development, avoiding blame for problems
- **Evidence from Munich LiMux**: "Real issues turned out to be different - it's all about managing change for and with people. It's about emotions."

**12. Works Councils and Trade Unions (especially ver.di)**
- **Role**: Employee representation in public sector, substantial workplace rights including veto power on some decisions
- **Power**: Medium - can block or significantly delay changes
- **Interest**: High - represent employee concerns
- **Concerns**: Job security, workload increases, adequate training, co-determination rights in technology decisions
- **Motivation**: Protecting members' interests, maintaining influence
- **Evidence**: Ver.di (1.9M members) specifically discusses AI and digital technologies at congress, has dedicated tech worker platform

**13. End Users (Government Employees)**
- **Role**: Daily users of government IT systems
- **Power**: Low individually, Medium collectively through resistance
- **Interest**: Medium to High
- **Concerns**: Usability, productivity impact, learning curve, compatibility with existing workflows
- **Motivation**: Ease of use, avoiding disruption to daily work
- **Evidence from Munich**: "Some users would fight back against change - especially if they regarded current system as good enough"

**14. Citizens**
- **Role**: Recipients of digital government services
- **Power**: Low directly, Medium through political pressure
- **Interest**: Low to Medium - focused on service quality not underlying OS
- **Concerns**: Service availability, data security (Germans particularly concerned about becoming "transparent citizens"), privacy protection
- **Motivation**: Better, faster digital services; data protection
- **Evidence**: Only 5% of Germans willing to use digital public services despite 93% shopping online, primarily due to security/privacy fears and poor usability

### External Influencers

**15. Major IT Vendors (Microsoft, SAP, Oracle)**
- **Role**: Current software providers with significant market presence
- **Power**: Very High - through technological lock-in, political lobbying, economic leverage
- **Interest**: Very High - protecting massive revenue streams
- **Concerns**: Loss of market share, precedent-setting for other governments
- **Motivation**: Revenue protection, maintaining vendor lock-in
- **Evidence**:
  - Federal government uses 32 cloud services, mostly US vendors
  - €4.6B Oracle contract signed without competitive bidding
  - Cloud costs tripled from €136M (2021) to €344M (2024)
  - Documentary claimed Munich's Windows return was personally motivated by mayor after Microsoft moved HQ to Munich
  - Microsoft demonstrates influence through data localization changes to satisfy German DPAs

**16. Open Source Community and Digital Sovereignty Advocates**
- **Role**: Technical advisors, implementation partners for FOSS solutions
- **Power**: Low-Medium (growing through successful examples)
- **Interest**: Very High
- **Concerns**: Government credibility after Munich reversal, sustainable funding, political commitment
- **Motivation**: Ideological (digital sovereignty, transparency), business opportunity for OSS companies
- **Evidence**: OpenDesk platform targeting 160,000 licenses by end 2025 across German public institutions

**17. EU Institutions**
- **Role**: Policy framework setter, interoperability requirements
- **Power**: Medium-High through regulatory requirements
- **Interest**: Medium - interested in digital sovereignty across EU
- **Concerns**: Interoperability across EU, strategic autonomy from US tech
- **Motivation**: European digital sovereignty, single digital market

**18. Professional Associations (e.g., Gesellschaft für Informatik - GI)**
- **Role**: Expert voice, policy recommendations
- **Power**: Low - advisory only
- **Interest**: High - professional standards and public sector IT quality
- **Concerns**: Growing digital dependency, ignored warnings about sovereignty risks
- **Motivation**: Professional standards, German technological competitiveness
- **Evidence**: GI and expert groups "repeatedly raised concerns" about digital dependency that were "largely disregarded"

## Stakeholder Dynamics and Conflicts

### Key Tensions:

**1. Federal vs. State Autonomy**
- Federal government wants standardization and coordination
- States want to preserve decision-making autonomy
- Resolution mechanism: IT Planning Council provides coordination, but decisions require consensus

**2. Security vs. Usability**
- BSI prioritizes security and sovereignty
- Users prioritize familiar interfaces and ease of use
- End users may resist secure but less familiar systems

**3. Cost vs. Sovereignty**
- Finance ministries focus on immediate costs
- BSI/digital sovereignty advocates emphasize strategic risk
- Vendor dependencies create hidden long-term costs (tripling cloud costs evidence)

**4. Political vs. Technical Decision-Making**
- Technical experts recommend reducing vendor dependency
- Political leaders respond to vendor lobbying and risk aversion
- Munich case shows political considerations can override technical success

**5. Coordination vs. Local Adaptation**
- IT Planning Council wants interoperability standards
- Municipalities need solutions fitting local capacity
- Gap: only 100 of 700 services digitized despite mandates

### Potential Champions:

1. **BSI** - strong security/sovereignty mandate, regulatory authority
2. **Schleswig-Holstein leadership** - proven commitment, successful implementation
3. **Digital sovereignty advocates** within federal government
4. **Data protection commissioners** - privacy concerns align with sovereignty
5. **Open source community** - technical expertise and implementation capacity

### Potential Blockers:

1. **Major vendors (Microsoft, SAP)** - massive economic interest in status quo
2. **Risk-averse political leadership** - fear of Munich-style failure
3. **State governments prioritizing stability** - not all states like Schleswig-Holstein
4. **Works councils** - if inadequate training/change management
5. **End users** - resistance to unfamiliar systems
6. **Finance ministries** - if migration costs emphasized over long-term savings

## Unique German Context Factors

### Federal Structure Impact:
- 16 states have constitutional autonomy for their own IT decisions
- Coordination requires consensus, not top-down mandate
- IT Planning Council structure reflects federal compromise
- Successful implementation requires state-by-state buy-in

### Coalition Politics:
- Multi-party coalitions mean competing priorities within government
- Digital ministry creation (2025) shows elevated priority but also fragmentation risk
- Political cycles create continuity risk (Munich reversal after political shift)

### German Political Culture:
- Strong data protection tradition (post-Third Reich sensitivity about "transparent citizens")
- Privacy concerns stronger than most countries
- Legal/regulatory thoroughness (IT-Grundschutz framework)
- Professional associations (GI) have cultural legitimacy

### Public Sector Dynamics:
- Strong co-determination rights (works councils)
- Civil service employment protections
- Professional IT staff unions (ver.di)
- High standards for public accountability

## Stakeholder Management Implications

**Critical Success Factors:**

1. **Secure IT Planning Council consensus** - binding decisions require all major stakeholders aligned
2. **Leverage BSI authority** - security mandate provides compelling rationale
3. **Learn from Schleswig-Holstein** - demonstrate feasibility and build champion network
4. **Address works councils early** - co-determination rights mean involvement is mandatory, not optional
5. **Manage vendor influence** - transparency about lobbying, competitive procurement
6. **Invest in change management** - Munich lesson: technical success requires emotional buy-in
7. **Respect state autonomy** - federal government cannot mandate, must persuade
8. **Address budget realities** - municipalities need funding and support, not just mandates

**High-Risk Areas:**

1. Political shift during implementation (Munich lesson)
2. Inadequate training leading to user resistance
3. Vendor lobbying at multiple levels (federal, state, local)
4. Budget constraints at municipal level blocking implementation
5. Coordination failure between federal-state-local levels
6. Loss of political champions through elections

## Confidence

**High confidence** in stakeholder identification and power dynamics based on:
- Comprehensive documentation of German government IT structure
- Multiple real-world case studies (Munich, Schleswig-Holstein, Foreign Ministry)
- Official government sources (IT Planning Council, BSI)
- Academic research on German federalism and change management
- Recent developments (2024-2025) showing current dynamics

**Medium confidence** in predicting specific stakeholder positions because:
- Coalition politics create internal diversity
- State governments vary significantly in priorities
- Political cycles can shift leadership priorities
- Vendor influence tactics not fully transparent

## Caveats

1. **State-level variation**: This analysis identifies general stakeholder categories, but each of 16 states has unique political leadership, IT maturity, and priorities
2. **Municipal diversity**: 11,000+ municipalities have vastly different capacities and needs - cannot treat as homogeneous group
3. **Dynamic vendor landscape**: Cloud/digital sovereignty initiatives may shift vendor strategies and offerings
4. **Political timing**: Analysis current as of 2026; coalition changes or elections alter political stakeholders
5. **Individual leadership matters**: Munich case shows individual leaders (mayor, CIOs) can be decisive beyond institutional positions
6. **Informal networks**: Power/interest matrix captures formal positions; informal influence networks also significant
