# Solution: International Law and Precedent Implications

## Answer

A U.S. acquisition of Greenland would set a highly problematic precedent for 21st-century territorial sovereignty that would fundamentally undermine the rules-based international order, regardless of whether the transfer were conducted through ostensibly legal means. While international law does not absolutely prohibit consensual territorial transfers between states, such an acquisition by a great power in the current geopolitical context would:

1. **Signal the return of great power territorial expansion** after decades of international norms constraining such behavior
2. **Undermine self-determination principles** unless Greenland's population gave genuine, free consent through proper democratic processes
3. **Weaken the credibility of territorial integrity norms** that have prevented major wars of conquest since 1945
4. **Create precedent that other powers would invoke** to justify their own territorial ambitions
5. **Expose double standards** in how international law is applied between powerful and weak states

**Key Finding**: Even if technically legal through proper consent mechanisms, the acquisition would represent a watershed moment in international relations—legitimizing great power territorial expansion in an era when such actions have been largely taboo since the post-WWII order was established.

## Evidence

### 1. UN Charter Framework and Fundamental Principles

The post-WWII international order is built on two sometimes-conflicting principles enshrined in the UN Charter:

**Territorial Integrity** (Article 2(4)): All UN Members must "refrain from the threat or use of force against the territorial integrity or political independence of any state." This principle has been recognized as customary international law and forms a cornerstone of the modern international system.

**Self-Determination** (Article 1(2)): One of the UN's main purposes is to develop friendly international relations based on "respect for the principle of equal rights and self-determination of peoples."

**The Critical Tension**: As international law scholars note, there exists a fundamental tension between these principles. The ICJ has observed that territorial integrity generally takes precedence, except in cases of decolonization, alien subjugation, or foreign occupation. However, as a matter of law, territorial integrity still holds a central place even in self-determination contexts.

### 2. Self-Determination Requirements for Greenland

**Greenland's Legal Status**: Under the 2009 Self-Government Act, Denmark explicitly recognized "that the people of Greenland is a people pursuant to international law with the right of self-determination." This recognition is crucial—it means Greenland cannot be transferred without the genuine consent of its population.

**Legal Requirements for Legitimate Transfer**: According to international law experts:
- A treaty on cession would be invalid if the relinquishing state were coerced or if the transfer breached a peremptory norm (jus cogens)
- **Critical finding**: "The right of self-determination would be violated if Denmark ceded Greenland to the United States without popular support in Greenland in favour of such a transfer of territorial title, even if this were a completely peaceful process"
- As Danish Prime Minister Frederiksen correctly stated, "Greenland belongs to the Greenlanders"—it cannot be ceded without the consent of its people

**Greenlandic Public Opinion**: A January 2025 poll showed 85% of Greenlanders opposed joining the United States, with only 6% in favor and 9% undecided. This overwhelming opposition means any current transfer would violate self-determination principles.

**Referendum Requirements**: For any referendum to be legitimate under international law:
- Must be held in a peaceful environment
- Civil and political rights must be effectively protected
- Voter qualification based on reasonable criteria
- Clear referendum question
- Free from coercion or undue pressure
- Meets guarantees of universal, equal, and secret suffrage per Article 25(b) of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights

### 3. Historical Precedents and Their Limited Applicability

**Alaska Purchase (1867)**:
- Purchased from Russian Empire for $7.2 million
- Conducted through formal bilateral treaty (Treaty of Cession, March 30, 1867)
- Occurred in 19th-century context where territorial acquisition by purchase was common and accepted
- **Critical difference**: Pre-dated UN Charter (1945) and modern self-determination norms
- No consultation with indigenous Alaskan populations occurred
- Legal, final, and recognized under international law of that era

**U.S. Virgin Islands Purchase (1917)**:
- Purchased from Denmark for $25 million in gold
- Treaty ratified after Danish referendum (December 14, 1916) and by both parliaments
- Strategic wartime context (WWI, protection of Panama Canal)
- **Critical differences**:
  - Pre-dated UN Charter and self-determination norms
  - Denmark held referendum of its own population, not the Virgin Islanders
  - Colonial context where metropolitan powers could transfer territories
  - Occurred before decolonization established self-determination as a fundamental right

**Key Finding on Historical Precedents**: These 19th and early 20th-century purchases occurred under fundamentally different international legal frameworks. They preceded:
- The UN Charter (1945)
- The principle of self-determination as jus cogens
- Decolonization norms
- Modern human rights law
- Post-WWII prohibitions on territorial aggrandizement

As one legal scholar noted, the international system "was established by colonial powers following World War II, at a time when most of the Global South had not even begun decolonization." Using these colonial-era precedents to justify modern territorial transfers would contradict the entire evolution of international law over the past 75+ years.

### 4. Recent 21st-Century Precedents

**Crimea Annexation (2014)**:
- Russia annexed Crimea from Ukraine following a disputed referendum
- UN Security Council: Russia vetoed draft resolution S/2014/189 (13 votes in favor, China abstaining)
- UN General Assembly Resolution 68/262 (March 27, 2014):
  - 100 nations supported
  - 11 voted against
  - 58 abstained
  - Declared the referendum invalid and affirmed Ukraine's territorial integrity
  - Emphasized the referendum "having no validity, cannot form the basis for any alteration of the status"
- **International consensus**: Widely viewed as violating fundamental principles of international law, particularly territorial integrity and prohibition on use of force
- UN continues to pass resolutions condemning Russia's occupation (as recently as 2020 and 2023)

**Kosovo Independence (2008)**:
- ICJ Advisory Opinion (July 22, 2010) found Kosovo's declaration of independence "did not violate international law"
- **Key reasoning**: "The scope of the principle of territorial integrity is confined to the sphere of relations between States"
  - Territorial integrity doesn't apply to non-state actors declaring independence
  - No general prohibition on declarations of independence exists
- ICJ took narrow approach, avoiding broader questions about positive right to self-determination or remedial secession
- **Critical difference from Greenland**: Kosovo involved unilateral declaration by the territory itself, not transfer between great powers

**South China Sea Disputes**:
- Philippines v. China arbitration (2016) under UNCLOS
- Tribunal ruled overwhelmingly for Philippines (July 12, 2016)
- Declared China's nine-dash line and historic rights claims "have no legal basis under UNCLOS"
- Ruling is "final and binding" under UNCLOS Annex VII, Article 11
- **China rejected the ruling**, maintaining it was "null and void"
- Demonstrates enforcement challenges: international legal system lacks central enforcement mechanism
- **Key lesson**: Even clear international law violations by great powers face minimal consequences when the violator rejects jurisdiction

### 5. Precedent-Setting Implications

**Signal to International Community**:

A U.S. acquisition of Greenland, even if conducted with proper consent mechanisms, would send several dangerous signals:

1. **Great Power Territorial Expansion is Back**: After 75+ years of norms against territorial expansion, a major power successfully acquiring strategically valuable territory would mark a paradigm shift

2. **Differential Application of Law**: The contrast between:
   - International condemnation of Russia's Crimea annexation
   - Potential acceptance of U.S. Greenland acquisition
   - Would reinforce perceptions that international law applies differently to Western powers vs. others

3. **Precedent for Other Disputes**: Other territorial claimants would cite this precedent:
   - Russia could point to it regarding former Soviet territories
   - China regarding Taiwan or South China Sea claims
   - Any regional power with territorial ambitions
   - Even if legal distinctions exist, political impact would be significant

**Rules-Based International Order Impact**:

The current international order rests on core norms including "equality under international law, sovereignty, territorial integrity, and the prohibition of aggression." However, as of early 2026, "the international world is witnessing events that disrupt the principles of sovereignty and the rules-based international order."

Five pillars constitute this order:
1. **Territorial order** - protects national sovereignty and discourages wars of conquest
2. Global trade order
3. Financial order
4. Nonproliferation order
5. Human rights order

A U.S. acquisition of Greenland would directly undermine pillar #1, which enshrines "a prohibition against the forcible change of national borders" and aims to "protect national sovereignty and discourage wars of conquest."

**Great Power Competition Context**:

The acquisition would occur amid intensifying great power competition where:
- Russia remains "aggressively revisionist in Europe"
- Russia and China work with Iran and North Korea in an "axis of upheaval" to resist Western-dominated order
- "Fragmentation rises, military operations replace diplomatic missions and the rules-based international order loses ground"
- Six "global swing states" (Brazil, India, Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, South Africa, Turkey) maintain "multi-aligned" positions, declining to choose sides

In this environment, a U.S. territorial acquisition would likely:
- Push swing states further away from the Western order
- Validate Russian and Chinese criticism of Western "hypocrisy"
- Accelerate the erosion of multilateral institutions
- Encourage other powers to pursue territorial ambitions

### 6. Global South Perspectives

**Historical Context and Grievances**:

Global South nations view territorial sovereignty through the lens of colonial history:
- "International law played a central role in the colonial consolidation of territorial sovereignty and histories of imperial rivalry"
- The international system was established by colonial powers when most of the Global South hadn't begun decolonization
- "Political leaders and thinkers across the Majority World have long pressed the most powerful states to support an overhaul of the global system, but have largely met with a refusal by Western powers to facilitate meaningful reforms"

**Contributions Often Erased**:
- "Much of the international human rights system we see today — from the conventions to the declarations, and from the institutions to the mechanisms, was built by, or at least shaped by, States of the Global South"
- In the 1960s, countries like Jamaica, Liberia, Ghana, Philippines, and Costa Rica "fundamentally reoriented the international human rights system"

**Sovereignty Concerns**:
- "Western powers are reducing states in the Global South to pawns of either the West or the East by viewing Global South countries as passive agents"
- "The hierarchical international order imposed by Western countries has shaped political demands of the Global South"
- Global South countries "weigh the defence of core norms of the international society (sovereignty, territorial integrity, international law) against their perceived interests"

**Likely Response to U.S.-Greenland Acquisition**:

Global South nations would likely view a U.S. acquisition as:
- Confirmation of Western double standards on territorial sovereignty
- Resurgence of colonial-era power dynamics
- Evidence that international law serves powerful states' interests
- Weakening of norms they depend on to protect their own sovereignty
- Justification for strengthening ties with alternative power centers (China, Russia)

This would further erode support for the Western-led international order and accelerate the shift toward multipolarity.

### 7. UN Security Council Dynamics

**Veto Power and Great Power Privilege**:

The five permanent members (P5) of the UN Security Council—China, France, Russia, United Kingdom, United States—hold veto power over all substantive decisions, including:
- UN sanctions
- UN peacekeeping operations
- Membership admissions and expulsions
- Condemnations of territorial changes

**Critical Limitation**: The U.S., as a permanent member, could veto any Security Council resolution condemning its acquisition of Greenland. This creates a fundamental asymmetry:
- Russia's Crimea annexation was condemned (though Russia vetoed Security Council action)
- A U.S. action could face no Security Council consequences due to veto

**Legal Arguments Against Self-Interested Vetoes**:
- Some scholars argue "a veto exercised by a permanent member to shield that state's own manifest and prima facie aggression from condemnation and collective action by the Council is legally flawed"
- Article 27, paragraph 3, of the UN Charter stipulates "the party to a dispute shall abstain from voting"
- However, these arguments have not prevented P5 members from using vetoes in matters concerning their own interests

**General Assembly Alternative**:
- Like with Crimea, the issue could move to the General Assembly (which passed Resolution 68/262 on Crimea)
- General Assembly resolutions are non-binding but carry political weight
- Likely outcome: significant opposition but also many abstentions, reflecting global divisions

### 8. International Court of Justice Implications

**No Compulsory Jurisdiction**:
- The ICJ only has jurisdiction when states consent
- The U.S. could simply not consent to ICJ jurisdiction on this matter
- Even if a case were brought, enforcement depends on Security Council action (which U.S. could veto)

**Advisory Opinion Possibility**:
- The General Assembly could request an ICJ advisory opinion (as with Kosovo)
- Advisory opinions are non-binding but carry legal weight
- Would likely emphasize self-determination requirements
- Outcome would depend heavily on whether Greenland's population genuinely consented

### 9. Legitimacy Pathways and Requirements

**The Only Potentially Legitimate Scenario**:

A transfer could potentially be legitimate under international law ONLY if:

1. **Genuine Greenlandic Consent**: Free, informed referendum with overwhelming support (not the current 6% approval)
2. **No Coercion**: Absence of economic pressure, military threats, or political manipulation
3. **Proper Process**: Referendum meeting all international standards (ICCPR Article 25(b) requirements)
4. **Danish Agreement**: Treaty properly ratified by Danish parliament
5. **Transparent Negotiation**: Open process respecting Greenland's self-government status
6. **International Acceptance**: Recognition by major powers and regional organizations

**Current Reality**: None of these conditions are currently met:
- 85% Greenlandic opposition
- U.S. president has refused to rule out military force
- Danish government and Greenlandic leaders oppose the idea
- International community has expressed concern

### 10. Comparative Analysis: Levels of International Law Violation

**Most Severe** (Clear Violations):
- Acquisition by military force without consent → Flagrant violation of UN Charter Article 2(4)
- Economic coercion forcing transfer → Violation of sovereignty and self-determination
- Transfer without Greenlandic referendum → Violation of self-determination as jus cogens

**Moderate** (Problematic but Technically Legal):
- Transfer with minimal Greenlandic consent (e.g., 51% support) → Technically legal but normatively weak
- Economic incentives influencing referendum → Gray area between legitimate offer and coercion

**Least Severe** (Potentially Legitimate):
- Transfer with overwhelming Greenlandic support (e.g., 80%+ in free referendum) after years of transparent deliberation → Could be legal but still sets problematic precedent

**Critical Finding**: Even the "least severe" scenario would undermine international norms because:
- It legitimizes great power territorial expansion in modern era
- Other powers would cite it regardless of procedural differences in their cases
- It shifts international norms away from territorial status quo
- The geopolitical impact outweighs the legal technicalities

## Caveats

1. **Scenario Dependency**: The analysis assumes various possible scenarios (from military force to consensual referendum). The legal and normative implications vary dramatically based on how acquisition would occur.

2. **Evolving Norms**: International law is dynamic. What constitutes acceptable territorial transfer continues to evolve. Some scholars argue the post-WWII prohibition on territorial change may be weakening amid great power competition.

3. **Enforcement Limitations**: International law lacks centralized enforcement. As the South China Sea arbitration demonstrated, even clear legal rulings can be ignored by powerful states with limited consequences.

4. **Geopolitical Context**: The assessment occurs during a period of heightened great power competition and challenges to the liberal international order. In a different geopolitical context, the implications might differ.

5. **Hypothetical Nature**: This analysis examines a hypothetical scenario. Actual events could unfold in unexpected ways that change the legal or normative calculus.

6. **Self-Determination Complexity**: The principle of self-determination itself is contested—when it applies, how it relates to territorial integrity, and what threshold of consent is required remain subjects of international legal debate.

7. **Time Horizon**: Short-term legal/diplomatic consequences may differ significantly from long-term impacts on international norms and state behavior.

8. **Regional Variation**: Different regions may react very differently based on their own historical experiences, security situations, and relationships with the involved parties.
